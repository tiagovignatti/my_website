Winner · Prêmio Jabuti Acadêmico 2025

What are the limits of rational thought? How did that question give birth to the computer? And what does computation really mean?

The Machine of Nature

A chronological journey through theoretical computer science.

The book

The Machine of Nature introduces the field of theoretical computer science in an unusual way — not as a branch of engineering, but as a discipline that reaches into philosophy and the natural sciences.

Told in three parts — past, present, and future — it explores the limits of manipulating information, the connection between computation and human thought, the parallels between the abstract and physical worlds, the tension between determinism and randomness, and much more.

Written entirely without technical jargon and structured as a story that follows the history of the field, it is made for everyone: the enthusiast, the expert, and the simply curious.

Theoretical computer science — the study of what can be computed, how efficiently, and what lies permanently beyond reach. The mathematics behind the machine.

Three acts: past, present, future

The story is told in order — chronology is the book's spine. Reading the parts in sequence is the point; skipping ahead is like reading spoilers.

I
The past

How and why computer science was born.

From the search for truth in Ancient Greece to the limits of formal reasoning.

II
The present

The classical era of deterministic computation.

The machine as a precise, predictable engine of logic.

III
The future

The modern era, where determinism gives way to randomness.

Chance and probability enter computation, and it no longer marches in a straight line.

Depending on where you stand, the three parts are also the distant past, the recent past, and the present of the field.

About the author

André Vignatti is an Associate Professor of Computer Science at the Federal University of Paraná (UFPR), in Curitiba, Brazil, where he has taught since 2011. He holds a PhD in Computer Science from the University of Campinas (UNICAMP).

His research spans the design and analysis of algorithms, randomized computation, complex networks, and machine learning. The Machine of Nature grew out of his course "Great Ideas in Theoretical Computer Science."

In 2025 the book won the Prêmio Jabuti Acadêmico, one of Brazil's most prestigious book awards.

Read the author's preface

The purpose of this book is to introduce the fascinating field of theoretical computer science. My main intention was to highlight the philosophical and natural-scientific interpretation of the field. Although this interpretation is, in my view, the essence of the field, texts that make it explicit are rare. To that end I decided (1) to write without technical language and (2) to narrate the story chronologically. I believe these two choices set this book apart.

Within the university, theoretical computer science is usually presented with formal rigor, ordered by prerequisites. I don't disagree with that if the goal is to maximize the absorption of technical content. But it poses problems: excessive formalism hides the big ideas, and a non-linear story makes the "big picture" hard to see. Few texts make a real effort to convey the intuition behind the formalism — yet a complete understanding, with all its rigor, is only reached once the intuition is assimilated. So this book abandons technical language completely. The trade-off: it does not offer a formal understanding of its subjects. If that's your goal, read it alongside a technical text.

The subjects are presented in chronological order and can be read as a story. Understanding the historical context of an idea offers a perspective you'd never get from the order these topics normally appear in a course. The recommendation follows naturally: read the book in sequence, because skipping chapters is like receiving spoilers. That said, the text is long and dense, so as far as possible the chapters are self-contained, with side notes pointing back to earlier material where there's a dependency.

The story is divided into three parts, representing the past, present, and future of the field. The first discusses how and why computer science was created; the second presents the "classical" era of deterministic computation; the final part deals with the modern era, marked by the abandonment of determinism.

From those two design decisions I realized it was possible to go further — to challenge the prevailing technical view and show that computer science transcends its use as an isolated, applied science. Not its everyday use to solve problems in other fields, but its philosophical and natural-scientific interpretation. That idea of "transcendence" is present from the very beginning, when the philosophical motivation for the "search for truth" in Ancient Greece is introduced. Along the way many topics connect back to it: the limits of formal science, the limits of manipulating information, the link with human consciousness and thought, the parallels between the abstract and physical worlds, the theory of biological evolution, determinism and randomness, among others.

This book was conceived as a work of scientific communication, accessible to the general public. But theoretical computer science isn't as popular as, say, theoretical physics, so I expect those most interested will be computer science students and professionals — and people from related fields such as mathematics, physics, and biology, who can use it to get acquainted with the field without resorting to complex texts.

Finally, even after enormous effort to review it, the book may still contain errors, omissions, and inaccuracies. If you spot any — in content, grammar, or formatting — or have suggestions, please get in touch.

Make yourselves comfortable and enjoy the trip!

— André
